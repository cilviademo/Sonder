**FADE IN:**

**INT. A LONG WOODEN DRAWER — NO TIME OF DAY**

Centered. Symmetrical. The camera looks straight down into a shallow drawer lined in faded velvet the color of an old book.

Inside: three envelopes. Three colors of paper. DOVE GRAY. CORNFLOWER. PERSIMMON.

Each envelope is addressed in a different hand to the same name:
ELIAS T. REYNARD.

A pair of hands enters the frame. Brown, slender, the nails kept short. Older hands. The hands lift the dove-gray envelope first. Hold it. Set it down.

They lift the cornflower one. Hold it. Set it down.

They lift the persimmon one. Hold it longer.

We do not see the face. We do not need to.

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**
There are three letters in the drawer.

Beat.

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**
They were written in different decades by different people who never met one another.

The hands set the persimmon envelope back. Slowly. As if it cost something.

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**
They all begin with the same sentence.

The drawer slides closed from outside the frame. Wood on wood. The sound of an old desk.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. ELI'S APARTMENT — KITCHEN — MORNING**

A small kitchen above a barbershop. Linoleum floor that someone has tried, more than once, to make beautiful and given up. A single window over the sink, half-open, letting in the soft electric buzz of the barbershop sign downstairs even though it is morning and the sign is off.

ELI REYNARD (33, Black, the kind of handsome that has clearly cost him something) stands at the counter in a t-shirt and boxers. He is making coffee in a small French press. His movements are the movements of a man who has lived alone long enough to know exactly where every implement lives.

On the counter beside the French press: a houseplant in a ceramic pot. Hand-painted on the pot, in someone's careful brushstroke: COLIN.

Eli waters Colin from a small green watering can. He does not look at the plant while he waters it. He is looking at his phone, propped against a coffee mug, screen up.

On the screen: an open email. We do not see the body. We see the subject line:
WE THINK IT'S TIME.

Eli sets down the watering can. Picks up the phone. Reads the email again. Sets the phone face down on the counter. He pours his coffee. Drinks it standing up.

From below, faintly, we hear UNCLE D opening the barbershop. The familiar small symphony: keys in a lock, the door's brass bell, the click of the overhead light.

Eli's phone, face down, BUZZES. Once. He does not pick it up.

It buzzes again. Twice.

Then again. A small flurry.

He drinks his coffee. Looks at Colin.

**ELI**
(to the plant)
Yeah, I know.

He picks up the phone. Looks at the screen. Reads the latest message. Then types, with one thumb:
OK.

He sends it. Sets the phone down. Finishes the coffee.

**CUT TO:**

**EXT. ELI'S APARTMENT BUILDING — DAY**

Eli on the front stoop. He has changed. Inherited brown corduroy three-piece suit, two sizes too large. A small leather suitcase at his feet. Colin the houseplant in the crook of one arm.

He is waiting for something.

A man passes on the sidewalk — mid-fifties, Members Only jacket, eating sunflower seeds from a paper bag. He spits a shell into an empty Sprite can he is carrying. Glances at Eli. Notices the plant.

**MAN WITH SUNFLOWER SEEDS**
That's a nice plant.

**ELI**
Thanks.

**MAN WITH SUNFLOWER SEEDS**
Where you taking it.

**ELI**
Family thing.

**MAN WITH SUNFLOWER SEEDS**
Mm.

The man spits another shell. Does not move on. They stand together for a beat, in the way two men can stand together on a sidewalk in the morning without either of them being uncomfortable about it.

**MAN WITH SUNFLOWER SEEDS**
They gon' be glad to see you.

Eli looks at him. The man is not looking at Eli. The man is looking up the street, where, around the corner, a car has just appeared.

It is a Volvo. Clean. Silver. Driven by someone we cannot yet see.

The man finishes his sunflower seeds, folds the paper bag in half with the crisp efficiency of long practice, drops it in a trash can three feet away. He nods to Eli. Walks on.

Eli watches him go for a beat. Then turns back to the Volvo, which has pulled up to the curb.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. MARLOW'S VOLVO — MOVING — DAY**

MARLOW (35, Black, polo shirt with a small embroidered fox over the heart) at the wheel. He drives the way a man drives when he has been told, by his father probably, that the way you drive says something about you.

Eli in the passenger seat. Suitcase between his feet. Colin balanced on his thigh.

They are not talking. They have not talked since Eli got in the car. The silence is not uncomfortable. It is something they are both choosing.

On the dashboard: a coffee cup from a chain neither of us recognize. A small fox-shaped air freshener hanging from the rearview mirror. The fox is smiling slightly.

They pass a billboard. A personal injury lawyer in a navy suit, smiling in a way that looks like it hurts him to do.

**MARLOW**
She's been up since five.

**ELI**
Yeah?

**MARLOW**
Made the bread herself this time. Won't let me tell her about the bakery on Tenth.

**ELI**
Don't tell her about the bakery on Tenth.

**MARLOW**
I'm not gonna tell her about the bakery on Tenth.

They drive.

**MARLOW**
She's nervous.

**ELI**
She doesn't get nervous.

**MARLOW**
You haven't been here. She gets nervous.

Eli looks out his window. Suburbs now. Trees that have been pruned by people who care about pruning.

They pass another billboard. The same personal injury lawyer. Same smile. Same suit.

Eli does not register it. Or if he does, he does not say so.

**MARLOW**
Cal's looking forward to seeing you, too.

Beat. Eli does not respond.

**MARLOW**
(trying again, lighter)
I told him you'd probably want to settle in first. Before, you know.

**ELI**
Before what.

**MARLOW**
Before.

Marlow shrugs, very slightly. Eyes on the road. Eli looks at him. Looks at the small fox-shaped air freshener. Looks back out his window.

**CUT TO:**

**EXT. THE REYNARD HOUSE — DAY**

A three-story Italianate, pear-yellow, slate-roofed. A brass fox weathervane catches the light. The lawn is geometrically perfect. A single leaf falls from a high tree, taking longer to land than physics should allow.

The Volvo pulls up to the gate.

Marlow shifts to park. Does not get out. Looks at Eli.

**MARLOW**
Take your time.

Eli nods. Does not move.

Marlow waits.

After a beat, Eli opens the door. Gets out with Colin. Reaches back in for the suitcase. Closes the door.

The Volvo pulls away. Eli watches it go.

He turns. Looks at the house.

HOLD on Eli's face. He is reading the house the way a man reads a contract before he signs it.

The front door opens. Inside the doorway, framed perfectly: AUNT CORDELIA REYNARD (71, Black, marigold cardigan, holding a small tray with a single cup of tea on it). She has been waiting at the door. Possibly since five.

**CORDELIA**
You're late.

**ELI**
By eleven years.

**CORDELIA**
By forty-five minutes. The eleven years I'd already accounted for.

She turns and walks back into the house. Eli follows. The door closes behind him.

HOLD on the empty doorway. The pear-yellow exterior. The brass fox weathervane, which catches the wind, turns half a degree, settles.

Above the doorway, very small, on a brass plaque we have not noticed before:
EST. 1890. THE REYNARDS.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. THE REYNARD HOUSE — GRAND PARLOR — CONTINUOUS**

A room arranged like a museum diorama of a room. Every object placed by someone who believes objects have feelings.

On a side table: a taxidermied owl on a small perch. A brass plaque at the base of the perch reads, in careful engraving: HORACE.

Cordelia sets down the tea on a low table between two leather chairs. Eli stands in the doorway. Suitcase still in his hand. Colin still in the crook of his arm.

**CORDELIA**
(not turning)
Sit, baby.

Eli sets down the suitcase. Carries Colin to the side table. Sets the plant down beside Horace the owl.

He sits. Cordelia sits across from him. She watches him for a beat. Then she pours.

**CORDELIA**
I made it the way you used to like.

**ELI**
How did I used to like it.

**CORDELIA**
With a little honey.

She hands him the cup. He takes it. He does not remember liking it with honey. He does not say so.

He sips. It is, in fact, good.

Cordelia watches him. She is doing something with her face that we cannot quite name. Possibly pleasure. Possibly a calculation, the kind a person makes when they have been waiting a long time for a small confirmation that the person they remember is still inside the person who has come home.

**CORDELIA**
Your great-uncle Caleb has decided to retire.

**ELI**
From what. He doesn't do anything.

**CORDELIA**
From the family. He is retiring from the family.

Eli's cup pauses on the way to his mouth. Just for a beat. He sets the cup back down on the saucer without drinking.

**ELI**
People can do that.

**CORDELIA**
Apparently. He's drafted documents.

She produces, from the deep pocket of the marigold cardigan, a thick cream-colored folder tied with a length of velvet ribbon the same persimmon as one of the envelopes from the prologue. She sets it on the table between them.

Eli does not touch it. He looks at it. Horace the owl, on the side table, possibly looks at it too.

**ELI**
What is it.

**CORDELIA**
An inheritance.

Beat.

**CORDELIA**
(the smallest smile)
Mostly of furniture.

Eli looks at her.

The grandfather clock in the corner does not tick. It whispers.

HOLD on Eli's face.

**[ACT TWO]**

**FADE IN:**

**INT. UNCLE D'S BARBERSHOP — AFTERNOON**

The barbershop is a sanctuary of analog warmth. The smell of talc and Barbicide. The hum of clippers.

UNCLE D (60s, wearing a crisp white smock) is sweeping the floor around an empty chair. He moves with the slow, deliberate grace of a man who has never been in a hurry a day in his life.

The brass bell above the door RINGS.

Eli enters. He is still wearing the oversized corduroy suit. He looks tired.

Uncle D stops sweeping. He leans on the broom. He looks at Eli for a long time.

**UNCLE D**
You look like you're wearing somebody else's clothes, son.

**ELI**
I am.

Uncle D chuckles, a low rumble in his chest. He points to the empty chair.

**UNCLE D**
Sit down. Let me fix your neck.

Eli sits in the chair. Uncle D drapes a cape over him and begins to work with the clippers.

**UNCLE D**
Heard you were back.

**ELI**
Word travels fast.

**UNCLE D**

**ELI**
He wants to give me something.

**UNCLE D**
Caleb?

**ELI**
Yeah. An inheritance.

**UNCLE D**
You gonna take it?

**ELI**
I don't know what it is yet.

**UNCLE D**
Yes, you do.

**ELI**
I haven't been here in eleven years, D. I don't know anything about what they're doing over there.

**UNCLE D**

**ELI**

*Bespoke Letterpress Stationers. For the sentences you haven't written yet.*

He locks the phone and sets it face down.

**UNCLE D**
Something wrong?

**ELI**
No. Just spam.

Uncle D brushes the loose hair from Eli's neck.

**UNCLE D**

**ELI**
Cordelia?

**UNCLE D**

**ELI**

**UNCLE D**
I know.

**UNCLE D**

Eli looks at the bottle. He does not touch it.

**CUT TO:**

**EXT. THE REYNARD HOUSE — EVENING**

The pear-yellow house is bathed in the soft, bruised light of magic hour. The shadows are long and deliberate.

**INT. THE REYNARD HOUSE — KITCHEN — CONTINUOUS**

Cordelia is at the stove, stirring a pot of something that smells like memory.

Eli enters. He stands in the doorway, watching her.

**ELI**

**CORDELIA**

**ELI**

**CORDELIA**

**ELI**

**CORDELIA**

**ELI**

**CORDELIA**

**ELI**
Does it?

**CORDELIA**

**ELI**

**CORDELIA**
I did.

**ELI**
What's the rest of it?

**CORDELIA**

**[ACT THREE]**

**FADE IN:**

**INT. ELI’S CHILDHOOD BEDROOM — NIGHT**

The room is dark, illuminated only by the small desk lamp. The light casts a warm, brassy glow over the stack of cream-colored paper.

Eli is sitting on the edge of the bed. He is holding the thick cream-colored folder Cordelia gave him in the parlor. The persimmon velvet ribbon is still tied in a neat bow.

He stares at the knot. He traces the velvet with his thumb.

He pulls the ribbon. It comes undone with a soft whisper of fabric.

He opens the folder.

Inside: a single sheet of paper. It is not cream-colored. It is pale gold.

There is only one sentence typed on the page, perfectly centered. The font is from a manual typewriter, the ink slightly uneven.

*Dear Elias — you are not the only one writing your life.*

Eli stares at the sentence. He reads it again. And again.

He looks up from the paper. He looks around the room. The perfectly made bed. The centered poster. The blank paper on the desk.

He stands up. He walks to the desk. He places the pale gold paper next to the blank cream paper.

He picks up the fountain pen. He holds it over the blank paper.

He waits.

He does not write anything.

He sets the pen down.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. THE REYNARD HOUSE — HALLWAY — CONTINUOUS**

Eli steps out of his bedroom. The hallway is quiet. The floorboards do not creak.

He walks toward the stairs. As he passes Cordelia's room, he notices the door is slightly ajar.

A sliver of light spills into the hallway.

Eli stops. He looks through the crack.

**INT. CORDELIA'S ROOM — CONTINUOUS**

Cordelia is sitting at a small table. On the table is a green portable typewriter.

She is typing. Slowly. Deliberately.

Next to the typewriter is a stack of pale gold paper.

Eli watches her. He does not move. He does not breathe.

Cordelia stops typing. She pulls the paper from the machine. She reads what she has written.

She reaches for a persimmon velvet ribbon.

Eli steps back from the door. He retreats into the shadows of the hallway.

He turns and walks quietly down the stairs.

**INT. THE REYNARD HOUSE — GRAND PARLOR — CONTINUOUS**

Eli enters the parlor. The room is dark, save for the moonlight filtering through the windows.

Horace the owl sits on his perch.

The grandfather clock whispers in the corner.

Eli walks to the clock. He stands in front of it. He listens.

*Forthcoming. Forthcoming. Forthcoming.*

He looks at Horace. The owl's glass eyes reflect the moonlight.

Eli turns away from the clock. He walks to the low table where the tea tray still sits. The two cups are empty.

He picks up his cup. He looks at the bottom. A small residue of honey remains.

He sets the cup down.

He walks to the front door. He opens it.

**EXT. THE REYNARD HOUSE — CONTINUOUS**

Eli steps out onto the porch. The night air is cool.

The brass fox weathervane is still.

Eli looks out at the geometrically perfect lawn. He looks at the street.

A car drives slowly past the house. It is a Volvo. Clean. Silver.

It does not stop. It continues down the street and disappears around the corner.

Eli watches it go.

He stands on the porch for a long time. The silence is absolute.

**[ACT FOUR]**

**FADE IN:**

**INT. ELI’S CHILDHOOD BEDROOM — MORNING**

The morning light is sharp and unforgiving. Eli is awake, fully dressed in his own clothes—jeans and a plain t-shirt. The oversized corduroy suit hangs over the back of a chair, discarded.

He looks at the desk. The pale gold paper with the single typed sentence still sits next to the blank cream paper.

He does not take the pale gold paper. He leaves it exactly where it is.

He picks up his suitcase and walks out the door.

**INT. THE REYNARD HOUSE — KITCHEN — CONTINUOUS**

Cordelia is at the table, drinking coffee from a delicate china cup. She is wearing a different cardigan today, this one the color of cornflower.

**CORDELIA**
You're leaving.

**ELI**
I have to get back to the city. I have a session booked.

**ELI**
Fine.

Aunt Cordelia.

Yes, Elias.

**ELI**
Did you type that letter?

**CORDELIA**
Which letter?

**ELI**
The one in the folder. The pale gold one.

Cordelia looks at him. Her expression is unreadable.

**CORDELIA**

**ELI**
That's not an answer.

**CORDELIA**

**ELI**

**CORDELIA**

**ELI**

**[ACT FIVE]**

**FADE IN:**

**EXT. TRAIN STATION — DAY**

The station is old, built of heavy stone and wrought iron. It feels like a place where time moves slower than it should.

Eli stands on the platform. He is holding his suitcase and Colin the houseplant.

The platform is mostly empty. A few commuters stand scattered along the edge, reading phones or staring blankly at the tracks.

An OLDER MAN (70s, wearing a faded Members Only jacket) sits on a bench nearby. He is eating sunflower seeds from a paper bag. He spits a shell onto the tracks.

Eli looks at the man. The man does not look back.

The sound of an approaching train rumbles in the distance.

Eli looks down at his phone. He opens his banking app.

The screen loads.

Checking Account Balance: $3,847.22.

He stares at the number. It is not enough to live on for long. It is not enough to fund a studio session.

He swipes to his recent transactions.

A deposit from two days ago: $5,000.00.
The description reads: *Continental Society Arts Grant - Disbursed via Reynard Institute.*

Eli stares at the screen. The realization hits him slowly, a cold weight settling in his stomach.

The train pulls into the station with a heavy screech of brakes. The doors open with a pneumatic hiss.

Eli does not move. He stares at the phone.

The commuters board the train. The Older Man stands up, folds his paper bag, and boards the train.

The doors close. The train pulls away.

Eli is left standing alone on the platform.

He looks at the suitcase in his hand. He looks at Colin the houseplant.

He looks back toward the town. Toward the pear-yellow house. Toward the glass and concrete building of the Institute.

He turns off his phone. He puts it in his pocket.

He turns around and begins to walk back the way he came.

**INT. THE REYNARD INSTITUTE — LOBBY — DAY**

The lobby is quiet. The Muzak version of "My Favorite Things" continues its endless loop.

Miriam is at the reception desk, typing.

The front doors open. Eli enters. He is still carrying his suitcase and the plant.

Miriam stops typing. She looks up. She smiles her polite, practiced smile.

**MIRIAM**
Elias. Did you forget something?

Eli walks to the desk. He sets the suitcase down. He sets Colin on the counter.

**ELI**
I need to see Marlow.

**MIRIAM**
Marlow is in a meeting.

**ELI**
Then I'll wait.

Miriam looks at him. She looks at the plant.

**MIRIAM**
Of course. Please, take a seat.

She gestures to a row of uncomfortable-looking chairs against the wall.

Eli walks to the chairs. He sits down.

He looks at the framed photographs on the wall. He looks at the fluorescent lights humming overhead.

He settles in to wait.

**END OF ACT FIVE**

**FADE IN:**

**INT. THE REYNARD HOUSE — BASEMENT ARCHIVE — NO TIME OF DAY**

A room we have not seen before. It is vast, lined with metal shelving units stretching into the darkness. The air smells of old paper and dust.

The shelves are filled with identical archival boxes. Thousands of them.

Cordelia walks slowly down an aisle. She is carrying the thick cream-colored folder with the persimmon velvet ribbon.

She stops in front of a specific box. The label on the box reads:
ELIAS T. REYNARD — VOL. 14.

She opens the box. It is filled with files, photographs, and loose papers.

She unties the persimmon ribbon. She opens the folder.

She takes out the pale gold paper with the single typed sentence.

She places the pale gold paper into the box.

She closes the box.

She turns and walks back down the aisle, disappearing into the shadows.

The camera lingers on the box.

ELIAS T. REYNARD — VOL. 14.

The faint, almost-inaudible hum of the building settling into itself.
